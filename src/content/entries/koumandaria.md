## Description / What we see

The wine is dark gold to amber, thick in the glass, with aromas of dried fig, raisin, and a faint resinous note. It is sweet but balanced by a quiet acidity that keeps it from cloying. In the old houses one still finds it served from heavy glass decanters that have turned slightly smoky with age.

## Observations & Context

<Observer> recorded that the best <Term name="Koumandaria" /> came from villages on the southern slopes of the Troodos, where the grapes could be dried on the flat roofs in the strong September sun. The process concentrates the sugars without the addition of spirit. The wine was already famous when the island passed between the great maritime powers.

## Deeper Notes

Production methods have changed little in their essentials. The must is fermented and then aged, sometimes in oak, sometimes in the cool dark of village storerooms. Its reputation travelled with the Knights and later with the merchants of Venice. For centuries it was one of the few Cypriot products that reliably reached the tables of Europe.

## Language Notes

The name descends from the French “Commanderie.” During the rule of the Knights Hospitaller the estates that produced the wine were administered by a commander. Over time the administrative title became the name of the wine itself. In Greek it is written Κουμανδαρία, yet the older pronunciation still lingers in some villages as “Komandaría.”
