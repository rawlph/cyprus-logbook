## Description / What we see

From the high ridges one sees the bones of the island: pillow lavas, sheeted dykes, and peridotite that has been altered into a pale, almost serpentine green. The rocks are not the usual limestone of the coasts. They feel older, heavier, more metallic when struck.

## Observations & Context

The <Term name="Troodos" /> massif rises in the centre of Cyprus. Its rocks were once part of the oceanic crust and upper mantle, formed at a spreading centre some 90 million years ago. Later tectonic forces pushed this fragment upward and onto the continental margin. [Geologist] recorded the first systematic mapping in the middle of the last century.

## Deeper Notes

Ophiolites are rare windows into the deep earth. The Troodos sequence is unusually complete and has become a global reference. Chromite deposits once supported mining communities. The same rocks now feed the rivers that carry sediment to the southern plains. The high elevation also creates the island’s only reliable summer coolness and its remaining forests.

## Language Notes

“Ophiolite” itself comes from the Greek ὄφις (óphis), “serpent,” because the altered peridotite often has a scaly, snake-skin appearance. Local names for particular outcrops are older still and survive in shepherd paths and spring names.
