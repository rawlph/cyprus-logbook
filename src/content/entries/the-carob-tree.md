## Description / What we see

The carob tree stands with a broad, rounded crown of dark evergreen leaves. Its trunk is often gnarled and low-branching, as if it has spent centuries deciding how best to hold the weight of its long, leathery pods. In late summer and autumn the pods turn from green to a deep, polished brown. They hang in thick clusters, heavy and slightly curved, like dark fingers.

## Observations & Context

Carob grows readily on the lower slopes and in the dry valleys of Cyprus. It tolerates drought and poor soils where many other trees struggle. [Observer] noted stands of old carob near the edges of abandoned terraces above the Akamas, their pods still gathered by families for winter feed. Goats and donkeys have always been fond of the sweet pulp.

## Deeper Notes

The tree belongs to the legume family. Its deep root system helps stabilise slopes. In times of scarcity the pods were ground into flour or roasted as a coffee substitute. The seeds inside are famously consistent in weight; this natural reliability gave the world the word <Term name="carob" /> and the carat.

## Language Notes

The Greek name χαρούπι (charoúpi) is ancient in feel. The path from the seed to the modern unit of purity for gold is one of the quieter linguistic journeys of the Mediterranean. In village speech one still hears affectionate diminutives for the smaller, wilder trees that grow in the maquis.
