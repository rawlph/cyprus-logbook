The south coast of Cyprus holds a quiet record of the island’s birth. The very same sea that once helped build this pale, gritty rock is now patiently taking it apart again — grain by grain, hole by hole. Walk these low cliffs or raised terraces and you are standing on an old shoreline that has been lifted into the light, then handed back to the waves.

## Description

When you walk across these outcrops the surface feels like steeled sand under your boots — firm, yet with a certain give. Freshly broken pieces feel gritty and partially crumble between your fingers. Flick a finger against a clean face and you get a surprisingly metallic, hollow sound. The rock captures the sun beautifully, glowing even in low light. In places the surface is pocked with rounded hollows and deep alcoves — the tafoni that give these outcrops their almost architectural look. Strike a fresh piece with another stone or a hammer and it rings with a sharp, slightly metallic note — the sound of its sand-sized grains shifting inside the cement.

## Observations

Locals and guides on Cyprus are rarely shy about sketching the island’s topography for you. Stand on these south-coast cliffs and they will point out the main features right away.

To the south rises the broad Troodos massif, the island’s central mountain heart. To the north runs the long, serrated Kyrenia Range. Between them lies the wide central lowland known as the Mesaoria — the plain that stretches east to west across much of the island.

The Mesaoria refers mainly to this interior basin and its flat to gently rolling lowlands between the northern and southern ranges. The coastal plateaus and cliffs we are standing on sit on the uplifted southern edge of the same ancient structural depression.

Long ago a shallow seaway occupied the area between the two rising landmasses. Marine sands collected there. Tectonic forces later lifted those deposits, exposing them as the cliffs and terraces along today’s coast.

How those sands became rock, and the forces now at work on it, is the subject of the deeper notes that follow. (This overview draws on walking the ground, conversations with locals and guides, standard geological references, and some assistance in shaping the narrative.)

## Deeper Notes

Calcarenite is the carbonate version of a sandstone. More than half its volume is made of sand-sized (0.06–2 mm) grains of calcium carbonate rather than quartz. “Carbonate” simply means the material comes from the hard parts of sea life — shells, tiny skeletons, and algae. The same mineral that makes up a seashell or ordinary limestone.

### Formation

These are not whole fossils stacked neatly on top of one another like a pile of plates. On the ancient seafloor, waves and currents ground up the shells and skeletons into sand-sized bits, then moved that sand around exactly the way beach sand moves today — piling it into shoals and dunes.

The beds themselves are typically cross-bedded and medium- to coarse-grained, interbedded with thinner layers of sandy marl. The grains are mostly bioclasts — fragments of benthic foraminifera (tiny single-celled marine organisms that build hard calcium-carbonate shells), coralline red algae (encrusting seaweeds that secrete carbonate skeletons), mollusks, and echinoids (relatives of sea urchins and sand dollars). This material collected in that shallow, high-energy seaway as the island was rising.

Once the loose sand was in place, cementation turned it into rock. Groundwater carrying dissolved calcium carbonate slowly seeped through the spaces between the grains. The mineral came out of solution and grew tiny calcite crystals that locked the grains together — like mortar setting between bricks. In many places the cement formed syntaxial overgrowths on echinoid plates: new crystals grew directly onto the original skeletal fragments, continuing their crystal structure rather than starting from scratch. Because burial was limited — the sediments were uplifted relatively quickly by the same tectonic forces that raised the Troodos ophiolite, the slice of ancient ocean crust that forms the core of Cyprus — the cement never completely filled every pore. That preserved porosity is what gives the rock its gritty feel, its sharp metallic ring when struck (the grains can still shift a little inside their calcite “glue”), and its readiness to let seawater and salts move through it later.

### Groundwater and Cementation

You might wonder where the groundwater comes from if all this sand was once sitting on the seafloor. When the grains were first piled up underwater, the tiny spaces between them were already filled with seawater trapped in the pores. As tectonic uplift raised the old seabed above sea level, rainwater and groundwater from the land began to seep downward through those same spaces. This water often carried dissolved calcium carbonate — picked up from dissolving shells or from the rock itself. As it moved through the sand and the chemistry changed (through slight evaporation or mixing), the dissolved mineral came out of solution and grew as tiny calcite crystals, gradually gluing the grains together like mortar between bricks.

Because the uplift happened relatively quickly, there wasn’t enough time or burial pressure for the cement to fill every pore. That left the rock porous — exactly the quality that later made it so susceptible to haloclasty.

### Why It Weathers and Sounds This Way

This combination of coarse grains and incomplete cementation makes the stone both historically useful — it was quarried and sawn for building in places like Nicosia — and vulnerable once it is exposed at the coast.

### Haloclasty Today

On the south coast you can watch haloclasty at work in real time. At the base of the cliffs, where spray regularly reaches the rock, fresh flakes of calcarenite lie on the ledges and the tafoni are sharp-edged and actively enlarging. Higher up, where wetting is less frequent, the hollows are smoother and older blocks have already detached, leaving scars on the cliff face. It is the movement of water through the grain framework — the same movement that once helped lock the sand into rock — only now working from the outside to loosen it again.




## Language Notes

The word “calcarenite” itself joins two simple ideas: Latin *calx* (lime) and *arena* (sand). It was coined for limestones that feel and break like sandstones. Locally people have long called it the calcarenite of the Athalassa or Nicosia beds — a plain name for a practical stone.

The sea’s current work has an equally plain name: *haloclasty* (salt breaking). The holes it carves are called *tafoni* (singular *tafone*). The word has a Greek ring to it — from *taphos* (τάφος), “tomb” or “sepulcher” — as though the sea were slowly carving small rock-cut chambers into the stone. (Some trace it instead to Corsican or Sicilian words for windows or perforations; the Mediterranean has always traded these names along with its shores.) Here the rock and the process have become inseparable. The very waters that once supplied the grains are now writing their signature into the cliffs with salt and wind.

## Summary

The calcarenite along the south coast of Cyprus is a relatively young rock, laid down roughly 5 to 2.5 million years ago in the shallow seaway that once filled the central basin. Waves and currents piled up sand-sized fragments of shells, foraminifera and other skeletal debris. Groundwater carrying dissolved calcium carbonate later seeped through the grains and cemented them with tiny calcite crystals — but never completely, which is why the rock remains porous and rings with a sharp metallic note when struck.

Sea spray now supplies salts that crystallize and expand inside the pore network — a process called haloclasty. This pries the grains apart from the outside, creating the rounded hollows and honeycomb patterns known as tafoni and, over time, causing larger blocks to detach from the cliff face.

The very features that make the calcarenite distinctive — its grain size, its incomplete cementation, and its resulting porosity — are also what make it especially susceptible to this kind of coastal weathering.
